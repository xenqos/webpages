Backdoor

A backdoor in poker is a term that refers to a situation where a player needs two consecutive cards on the turn and river to complete a hand. This is often used to describe situations where a player has a small chance of completing a hand but ends up doing so by getting the right cards on the turn and river.

The Concept of Backdoor in Poker

A backdoor draw is a poker term that describes a situation where a player needs both the turn and river cards to complete a winning hand. It's not the ideal situation to be in, as the odds of completing a backdoor draw are usually quite low. However, when it does happen, it can result in a strong hand that can take down the pot.

Backdoor draws are most common in Texas Hold'em and Omaha, where there are multiple community cards dealt throughout the hand. This gives players multiple opportunities to complete a backdoor draw.

The Strategy Behind Backdoor

Although a backdoor draw is not a strong hand to start with, it can sometimes be a good idea to stay in the hand if the pot odds are right. For example, if there are a lot of players in the pot and it's not too expensive to call, it might be worth it to see if you can hit your backdoor draw.

However, a key point to remember is that backdoor draws should not be the main part of your poker strategy. They are more of a plan B, in case your original plan doesn't work out. Relying on backdoor draws too often can lead to losing chips quickly.

Examples of 'Backdoor' in Poker

For example, let's say you're playing Texas Hold'em and you're dealt an Ace of Hearts and King of Hearts. The flop comes down 2 of Hearts, 7 of Clubs, and Jack of Spades. At this point, you have a backdoor flush draw, meaning if the turn and river are both Hearts, you would have a flush.

  • "Even though my hand wasn't strong, I decided to call because I had a backdoor straight draw."
  • "I was lucky and hit my backdoor flush on the river to win the hand."

Backraise

A 'Backraise' in poker refers to a re-raise from a player who originally called a bet. This is a powerful move used to trap aggressive players or to build the pot when a player has a strong hand.

What is a Backraise in Poker?

In poker, a 'Backraise' is a strategic move that involves a player first calling a bet and then re-raising when the action returns to them in the same betting round. This move can be used as a trapping play against aggressive opponents or as a way to build a larger pot when a player has a strong hand.

The Strategy of Backraising

Backraising can be a highly effective tactic when used in the right situations. It often comes as a surprise to opponents, which can cause them to make mistakes in their response.

Backraising is typically used in two main scenarios. The first is as a trapping play against aggressive opponents. By initially just calling, a player with a strong hand can induce further bets from aggressive opponents. When the action returns to them, they then re-raise, forcing their opponents to commit more money to the pot.

The second scenario is when a player wants to build a larger pot. By backraising, they can potentially get more money into the pot than they could by simply raising in the first place.

When to Backraise

Deciding when to backraise should be based on the specific circumstances of the hand. Here are a few situations when a backraise might be a good move:
You have a strong hand and you're facing an aggressive opponent who is likely to bet or raise.
You're in a late position and several players have already called the initial bet.
You have a strong hand and you want to build the pot.

Example of "Backraise"

Let's say you're playing a hand of Texas Hold'em and you're dealt a pair of aces. An aggressive player raises before the flop, and you decide to just call, hoping to induce further bets. Another player then also raises. When the action returns to you, you backraise, forcing your opponents to commit more money to the pot.

  • "With a pair of aces in hand, he decided to backraise to trap the aggressive player."
  • "She used a backraise to build a larger pot after hitting a set on the flop."
  • "Facing multiple opponents who had already called, he decided to backraise with his strong hand."

Bad Beat

A bad beat in poker refers to a situation where a player with what appears to be strong cards nevertheless loses. It often occurs when a player bets the stronger hand and their opponent makes a poor call that eventually wins with the turn or the river card.

Understanding Bad Beat

A bad beat can be a pivotal moment in a poker game. It's a term used to describe a highly favored hand losing to an underdog's hand that catches up and beats it. In essence, it's when a player loses a hand despite having the odds significantly in their favor.

The most common scenario is when a player goes all-in with a strong hand like aces or kings and gets called by a player with a lesser hand like 5-6 suited. The flop, turn, and river come out in a way that favors the lesser hand, resulting in a bad beat.

Bad beats are a part of the game, and handling them with grace is a part of being a good poker player.

Bad Beat in Online Poker and Live Poker

Whether you're playing poker online or live, bad beats are inevitable. Online, they might seem more frequent due to the speed of play. In live poker, bad beats can be more dramatic as players physically react to the loss.

Bad beat jackpots are popular in both online and live poker. They offer a consolation prize to the player who loses with a strong hand, often quad eights or better.

Examples of bad beat situations include:
You have a pair of aces, and your opponent has a pair of eights. The community cards come out, and your opponent ends up with four eights.
You have a straight, and your opponent has a lower straight. The river card gives your opponent a flush.

Example sentences of the term in use:

  • "I had pocket aces and still lost. Talk about a bad beat."
  • "He caught his only out on the river for a bad beat."
  • "I've had some bad beats, but losing with a full house was the worst."

Balanced Style

A style of play that avoids potentially exploitable patterns by performing different actions in similar situations. For example, a player who always makes a pot-sized bet with a strong hand becomes very predictable to attentive opponents, but by “balancing” his style with a variety of actions in the same situation (e.g., checking, betting small, betting half the pot, betting the whole pot), the player becomes more difficult to read.

 

Bankroll

A 'Bankroll' in poker refers to the total amount of money a player has set aside to play poker. It's an essential part of poker strategy, as it determines the stakes a player can afford to play and helps manage the inherent variance in poker.

What is a Bankroll in Poker?

In poker, a 'bankroll' is the total sum of money a player dedicates to playing poker. It's a separate fund from personal finances and is used solely for poker-related expenses. The size of a player's bankroll can influence their betting decisions, the stakes they play, and how they manage their money.

The Importance of Bankroll Management

Proper bankroll management is a crucial aspect of successful poker play. It's a risk management strategy designed to ensure that a player can withstand the natural swings and variance associated with poker without going broke.

Bankroll management involves playing within your limits and not risking more than a small percentage of your bankroll on any single game or tournament. This approach helps players stay in the game longer and ride out losing streaks.

Determining Your Bankroll

How much money you should have in your poker bankroll depends on several factors, including the type of poker you're playing (cash games vs. tournaments), your skill level, and your tolerance for risk. As a general rule, you should have enough in your bankroll to cover a reasonable number of buy-ins.

For example, if you're a cash game player, a common recommendation is to have a bankroll of at least 20-30 times the buy-in of the stakes you're playing. If you're a tournament player, you might want to have a bankroll of at least 100 times the buy-in of the tournaments you play.

Example of "Bankroll"

Let's say you're a regular player at $1/$2 No-Limit Hold'em cash games, where the typical buy-in is $200. Following the recommendation of having a bankroll of at least 20 times the buy-in, you should ideally have a bankroll of $4,000.

Bet Sizing

Bet sizing in poker is the act of determining the appropriate amount to wager in a particular situation. A crucial aspect of successful poker strategy, bet sizing can influence the outcomes and profitability of your poker games.

Understanding Bet Sizing

Bet sizing is not a one-size-fits-all concept in poker. It depends on multiple factors such as the pot size, opponent's tendencies, hand strength, and table dynamics. By adjusting your bet size according to these variables, you can maximize your expected value (EV) in the long run.

In cash games, common bet sizes range from half-pot to full-pot bets, while in tournament poker, players often use a smaller bet size due to the increasing blinds and antes. The goal of bet sizing is to construct a bet that gives your opponents a tough decision while minimizing your risk.

The Importance of Bet Sizing

Effective bet sizing can help you achieve various objectives. It can help you extract value from your strong hands, bluff effectively with your weak hands, and manipulate the pot size to your advantage. It's a crucial tool for controlling the flow of the game and keeping your opponents off balance.

Moreover, understanding bet sizing can help you identify the mistakes of your opponents and exploit them. For example, if an opponent consistently over-bets the pot with weak hands, you can adjust your strategy to profit from their poor bet sizing.

EXAMPLE OF "BET SIZING"

Let's say you're playing a $1/$2 no-limit hold'em cash game, and you've been dealt pocket aces on the button. A player in middle position opens the pot with a raise to $6. You decide to 3-bet to $20, which is a standard size in this situation.

In another situation, you're on the big blind with J-10 suited and the button raises to $6. You decide to call, making the pot $13. The flop comes J-7-2, giving you top pair. You decide to make a half-pot bet of $6.50 to extract value from weaker hands and protect your hand from potential draws.

  • "By adjusting my bet sizing, I was able to maximize the value of my strong hand."
  • "Understanding bet sizing helped me exploit my opponent's betting tendencies."

Big Blind

The term "Big Blind" is a mandatory bet made before the cards are dealt in a game of poker. It is generally the larger of two forced bets in a poker round and typically the player sitting two places to the left of the dealer puts in the Big Blind.

Understanding the Big Blind

The Big Blind is a crucial element of the betting structure in poker. It's designed to stimulate betting and ensure there's always a pot worth playing for. The Big Blind follows the Small Blind and is usually double the size of the Small Blind. However, the amount can vary depending on the game's stakes. The blinds are used in games like Texas Hold'em and Omaha, but not in variants like Seven-Card Stud.

In cash games, the value of the Big Blind remains constant and is listed as part of the table stakes. For example, in a $1/$2 game, the Big Blind is $2. In tournaments, however, the value of the Big Blind increases at regular intervals to ensure the tournament progresses towards a conclusion.

How the Big Blind Affects Strategy

The position of the Big Blind is a strategically significant one. This player is the last to act before the flop, which provides an opportunity to observe others' actions before deciding on their move. However, this position also carries the risk of being forced to contribute a significant amount to the pot, irrespective of the strength of their hand.

Playing from the Big Blind requires a good understanding of hand selection and post-flop play. Since the Big Blind has already invested in the pot, they often have better odds to see the flop, even with marginal hands. However, they also need to be capable of making tough decisions after the flop, as they will often be out of position for the rest of the betting rounds.

For example, if you are in the Big Blind with a weak hand and everyone folds to the Small Blind who raises, you are faced with a decision. You could fold and lose your Big Blind, call and see the flop, or raise in an attempt to steal the pot.

Examples:

  • "The player to the left of the small blind is required to post the big blind."
  • "The big blind in this tournament increases every 15 minutes."
  • "From the big blind position, you have the advantage of being the last to act pre-flop."

What is a Big Slick in Poker?

This is a term used to describe the Ace-King hand in Texas Hold’em Poker. It's often referred to as "big slick" because it's a big hand, but it can be slippery: it's got a lot of potential, but it could also lead to a big loss if not played correctly.

Understanding Big Slick

Big slick is a starting hand in Texas Hold'em Poker that consists of an Ace and a King. These are two of the highest ranking cards you can have as a starting hand, making this a very strong, although somewhat risky, hand to play.

The term comes from the fact that these two cards together can be very strong – big – but also very slippery or tricky to play – slick. While you have a good high-card strength, you don't yet have a pair or any other winning poker hand, which can make strategy complicated.

How to play the Big Slick

Playing a big slick well requires a balance of aggressive and cautious play. It is not a made hand yet, but it has the potential to turn into a powerful one after the flop (the first three community cards that are dealt after the first betting round). One of the potential dangers of big slick is that, because it's so high ranking, many players overvalue it and tend to bet too aggressively pre-flop.

Here's a list of steps to consider when you're dealt a big slick:

  • Pay attention to your position: If you’re in an early position, you could raise to scare off weaker hands.
  • Pay attention to the others: if the table is playing very aggressively, it can be smarter to fold a big slick pre-flop than to risk too much.
  • The number of players in the game: Big slick plays better in a game with fewer players.
  • The flop: If the flop gives you an Ace or a King, you’re in a strong position.

Example of "Big Slick"

For instance, if you're dealt an Ace and King, you have Big Slick. This hand has a good chance of becoming a high pair or a straight, but it could still be beaten by any made hand like a pair, two pairs, or three of a kind.

Blind

A Blind in poker is a mandatory bet that is placed by two players before any cards are dealt. There are typically two blinds in a hand: the small blind and the big blind.

What is a Blind?

In poker, a Blind is a forced bet that two players must make before any cards are dealt. There are two types of blinds: the small blind and the big blind. The small blind is usually half the size of the big blind, and is placed by the player to the left of the dealer. The big blind, placed by the player to the left of the small blind, is usually equivalent to the minimum bet.

Playing from the Blinds

Playing from the Blinds can be challenging because you will be out of position for the rest of the betting rounds. This means you will be one of the first to act, which can be a disadvantage as you have less information about what other players might do.

Considerations When Dealing with Blinds

When you're in the blinds, it's important to consider your hand strength, the betting action, and the tendencies of the other players. Since being in the blinds puts you at a positional disadvantage, you generally need a stronger hand to play compared to if you were in a later position.

Example of "Blind"

For instance, in a game with a small blind of $1 and a big blind of $2, the player to the left of the dealer would place a $1 bet before the cards are dealt, and the player to their left would place a $2 bet. This creates a starting pot of $3.

Bluffing plays a significant role in both cash games and tournaments, and is a strategy applicable to all poker variants, including Texas Hold'em and Omaha.

Bluffing is not just about the cards you hold, it's also about the reads you have on your opponents and your table image. Timing is crucial when bluffing. A well-timed bluff can win you a pot when your cards cannot.

In an Omaha game, you're dealt a hand that has little to no potential of winning. However, based on your read of your opponent and their potential hand, you decide to bluff and make a large bet, causing them to fold.

Understanding a Bluff Catcher

In the world of poker, a Bluff Catcher is a hand that has marginal value on its own but can prove useful when an opponent is likely bluffing. It's a hand that's not strong enough to bet for value, but it could still win at showdown if the opponent indeed was bluffing. Knowing when to use a bluff catcher can be a critical part of strategy, especially in games with a lot of aggressive players.

When to Use a Bluff Catcher

Understanding when to use a Bluff Catcher requires a strong understanding of your opponent's tendencies and betting patterns. If an opponent frequently bluffs, then your chances of successfully using a bluff catcher increase. Likewise, if your opponent is conservative and rarely bluffs, a bluff catcher becomes less effective. It's all about reading the table and making informed decisions.

You can only win if your opponent is bluffing and does not hold an Ace or 9.

Bomb Pot

The term "Bomb Pot" in poker refers to a special type of hand where all players at the table agree to put in a predetermined amount of money into the pot before the flop is dealt. After the bet, the hand proceeds as normal. Typically, bomb pots occur once every orbit at a table and are more common in cash games than tournaments.

Introduction to Bomb Pot Poker Rules

In Bomb Pot poker, all players agree to place a predetermined bet before the flop is dealt. This creates a larger initial pot, making the hand more exciting and potentially rewarding. Bomb pots are especially popular in cash games, although they can occur in tournaments as well. The key aspect that distinguishes a bomb pot from a regular hand is the mandatory pre-flop betting by all players.

Playing a Bomb Pot Poker Hand

When a bomb pot is declared, each player must put in the agreed-upon amount of money into the pot. This is done before the flop, and no player can opt out if they wish to participate in the hand. Once the bet is placed and the flop is dealt, the hand continues as normal. Players can check, bet, raise, or fold, depending on their hand and the strategy they wish to employ. It's important to note that bomb pots can lead to larger pots than typical hands, which can add a thrilling element to the game.

All players at a table agree to a $5 bomb pot. Each player puts $5 into the pot before the flop is dealt.

Bounty

A 'Bounty' in poker is a monetary prize awarded to a player who eliminates another player from the tournament. This prize is typically a portion of the eliminated player's buy-in and adds an extra layer of excitement and strategy to the game.

What is a Bounty in Poker?

In bounty tournaments, each player carries a bounty that gets claimed by whoever knocks them out. This means that apart from the traditional tournament prizes, players have the opportunity to win extra cash each time they eliminate an opponent. This feature adds an additional dimension of strategy to the game, as players might adjust their play to try and claim more bounties.

How do Bounties Work in Poker?

In a bounty tournament, part of your buy-in goes towards the tournament prize pool, and another part becomes your bounty. The exact split may vary, but a common structure is a 50/50 divide. So, if the buy-in is $200, $100 would go to the prize pool, and the other $100 becomes your bounty.

When you knock out another player, you win their bounty. In some tournaments, known as progressive knockout (PKO) tournaments, you only receive a portion of the bounty (typically 50%), and the rest gets added to your own bounty, making you a more appealing target for other players.

Strategies Around Bounties

Bounties can significantly influence the dynamics of a poker tournament. Players may be more willing to take risks if they have the opportunity to win a bounty, resulting in more aggressive play. Conversely, if you have a large bounty on your head, you might find yourself being targeted by other players.

Understanding the value of bounties and adjusting your strategy accordingly is crucial to succeeding in bounty tournaments. You need to carefully weigh the potential reward of claiming a bounty against the risk of losing chips or being eliminated from the tournament.

Example of a "Bounty"

Suppose you're in a bounty tournament with a buy-in of $200, and the bounty is set at $100. You're dealt a strong hand and are up against a single opponent on the flop. Considering the added incentive of the $100 bounty, you decide to go all-in. Your opponent calls, and you win, eliminating them from the tournament. You've just claimed your first bounty of the game.

  • "With a large bounty on his head, he became a prime target at the table."
  • "She adjusted her strategy to try and collect as many bounties as possible."

Burn Card

A burn card is a card that is removed from the top of the deck and discarded without being revealed to the players. This is done before community cards are dealt in games like Texas Hold'em and Omaha, as a means of ensuring fairness and preventing cheating.

What is a Burn Card?

A burn card is a common practice in both live and online poker games. It is the card that the dealer discards from the top of the deck, face down, before dealing the community cards. This is done in games like Texas Hold'em and Omaha to ensure fairness and prevent any form of cheating. The burn card is not revealed to the players and has no influence on the game's outcome.

The Purpose of the Burn Card

The primary purpose of the burn card is to protect the integrity of the game. In a live poker game, it's possible for players to mark the cards or gain some knowledge about the cards based on dealer's handling. By burning a card, it ensures that even if a player has gained some information about the top card, it will not affect the game as that card gets discarded.

A Burn Card in Different Poker Games

In Texas Hold'em and Omaha, the dealer burns a card before the flop, the turn, and the river. This means three cards are burned in total. In Seven-Card Stud, the dealer burns a card before dealing the final card. In draw poker games, no cards are burned.

For example, in a game of Texas Hold'em, once the first round of betting is complete, the dealer will burn one card and then deal the flop. After the second round of betting, another card is burned before dealing the turn, and the same happens before dealing the river.
